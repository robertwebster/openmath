# Open Math — Tone & voice guide

This document defines the writing voice for Open Math. Use it when drafting any user-facing prose: about pages, topic introductions, feedback messages, error states, button labels, empty states, and explanatory text.

---

## Core principle

Write like good documentation, not like a marketing site or a textbook.

The reader is a 12–18 year old student in NSW. Treat them as capable. Don't try to be cool. Don't try to be reassuring in a saccharine way. Be useful, be clear, be honest — that's the whole voice.

---

## The voice in one line

Plain, direct, slightly understated. Honest about what the project is and isn't.

---

## What the voice is

**Direct.** Get to the point in the first sentence. Don't warm up.

**Plain.** Use short words where they work. "Use" not "utilise". "Help" not "facilitate". "About" not "regarding".

**Honest.** Say what's true, including limitations. If content is AI-generated, say so. If a topic is hard, say so.

**Calm.** No exclamation marks except in genuinely exceptional cases — and even then, probably not. No hype words. No "amazing", "awesome", "incredible".

**Respectful of the reader.** Assume they're smart enough to handle plain information. Don't over-explain. Don't condescend.

**Slightly understated.** When in doubt, dial it down rather than up. "It's not bad" can do more work than "it's great".

---

## What the voice is not

- Not corporate. No "empowering learners on their journey".
- Not chummy. No "hey there!", no "let's dive in", no "you've got this!".
- Not academic. No "henceforth", no "it is important to note that".
- Not gamified. No "level up", no "unlock", no "crush this topic".
- Not anxious. Don't apologise constantly or hedge every claim.

---

## Concrete rules

### Sentences

- Short to medium. If a sentence runs past 25 words, look at it again.
- Vary length. A short sentence after two medium ones lands well.
- Fragments are fine when they earn their place. Don't overuse them.

### Word choice

- Prefer the simpler word. "Help" over "assist". "Show" over "demonstrate". "Try" over "attempt" (in UI copy; "attempt" is fine in prose about questions).
- Avoid filler: "really", "very", "just", "simply", "actually" — cut most instances.
- Avoid intensifiers and superlatives. The site doesn't have "the best" anything.
- Australian English spelling throughout: "practise" (verb), "practice" (noun), "behaviour", "centre", "organise".

### Person and address

- Use "you" for the student.
- Use "we" sparingly. The project speaks for itself most of the time. "We" is fine when it genuinely means the people behind the project — e.g. "We review every question against the syllabus."
- Don't refer to the student as "learners" or "users". They're students, or "you".

### Punctuation

- Avoid em dashes. Use a comma, full stop, or colon instead.
- No exclamation marks in normal prose.
- Oxford comma: use when it aids clarity, skip when it doesn't. Be consistent within a page.
- Contractions are encouraged: "don't", "you're", "it's". They make prose less stiff.

### Headings and labels

- Sentence case for headings, not Title Case. ("Linear equations", not "Linear Equations".)
- Buttons are verbs: "Start", "Try again", "Show working".
- No clever button labels. "Get started" not "Let's go!".

## Tone in specific contexts

### Topic introductions

State what the topic is, what the student is expected to know, and what they'll be doing. No motivational opener.

✓ *Linear equations are equations where the variable only appears to the power of one. This topic covers solving them, rearranging them, and graphing them on the number plane. By the end you should be able to handle the kinds of questions in your Year 9 topic test.*

✗ *Welcome to linear equations — one of the most exciting topics in Year 9! Get ready to dive in and master this fundamental skill!*

### Correct-answer feedback

Acknowledge briefly. Don't celebrate.

✓ Correct.  
✓ Correct — that's the standard form.  
✗ 🎉 Awesome work! You nailed it!

### Incorrect-answer feedback

Be specific where possible. Don't be soft. Don't be harsh either.

✓ *Not quite. The sign on the second term flipped when you moved it across — try that step again.*  
✗ *Oops! That's not right, but don't worry, you'll get it next time!*  
✗ *Incorrect.*

### Error states and missing content

Plain, honest, no apology theatre.

✓ *This page doesn't exist. Try the syllabus index.*  
✗ *Oops! Something went wrong. We're so sorry for the inconvenience!*

### About and project info

Honest about what the project is, including the AI-assisted content. Match the reference paragraph below.

---

## A quick test

Before publishing any prose, read it back and ask:

1. Would a 14-year-old understand this without effort?
2. Does it sound like a real person wrote it, or like a brand?
3. Could any sentence be shorter or simpler?
4. Is there a hype word, filler, or motivational phrase that can be cut?
5. Does it state things plainly, or hedge?

If the answer to any of these is wrong, edit.

## Reference paragraph

Use this as the calibration sample. New prose should feel like it could sit next to this without standing out.

> Open Math is a free maths site built around the NSW syllabus for Years 7 to 12. No accounts, no subscriptions, no ads — just the topics you're actually being assessed on, with questions you can attempt and get feedback on straight away. Content is written with AI assistance and reviewed by a human against the syllabus, and we say so plainly because that's more honest than pretending otherwise. The goal isn't to cover everything under the sun. It's to help you walk away from a topic feeling a bit less stuck than when you started.

If a piece of writing matches the rules above and sits comfortably next to this paragraph, it's on tone.
